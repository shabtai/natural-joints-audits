Audit by Natural Joints
Headline: no published claim of this paper is affected. A full audit of the deposited proteomic matrix, clinical tables, multiplexed-imaging tables, and the released image-analysis code found zero claim-invalidating defects. Every issue surfaced is a bounded caveat — a limitation that, when tested against the specific claim it could undermine, leaves the paper’s conclusions standing. We report them here in the interest of transparency and to flag the data’s soft spots for anyone re-analysing the public deposit.

The paper’s core conclusions — grade- and subtype-associated proteomic differences, higher proteomic intra-tumor heterogeneity in high-grade tumors, and the imaging–proteomics integration — survive every targeted check. Two of the four claim-level caveats are by design (the authors already mitigate them); the other two are statistical-fragility notes, not errors.

Methodology — how the checks were localized

Auditing an entire multi-omics paper claim-by-claim is intractable, and the paper’s downstream statistical code was not deposited (only the upstream QuPath image-analysis scripts are public), so the figures cannot be re-run verbatim. Instead we ran automated semantic enrichment over the deposited data. The enrichment profiles every table, resolves column meanings and grain, and emits a structured set of risk signals: grain/roll-up hazards, batch-confound co-occurrence edges, missing-data and completeness gaps, identifier ambiguities, and scale/distribution checks.

Those signals act as a map. Each flagged risk was matched to the specific published claim it could undermine, and then tested with a targeted statistical reproduction on the deposited inputs (the proteomic matrix + the experimental-design metadata). Claims the enrichment did not flag were not audited.

This is the efficient way the enrichment paid off: instead of auditing the whole paper, it localized the ~8 places worth checking — and each, on inspection, resolved to a bounded caveat rather than a defect. Claim-level caveats (verified; none reverse a conclusion)

Caveat 1 — Subtype/grade differential proteins are structurally confounded with TMT batch, but survive a batch-aware model
VERIFIED NO CLAIM REVERSAL
ClaimFig 1 — proteins differential between TNBC/HR+ and Grade 2/3 TestCramér’s V; linear mixed model with TMT set as a random effect; within-patient control

Receptor subtype and grade are strongly associated with the TMT multiplexing batch (Cramér’s V = 0.69 for subtype, 0.76 for grade); several TMT sets are 100% one subtype. Because subtype/grade is a patient-level property and patients were largely assigned to subtype-pure TMT runs, batch and biology cannot be perfectly separated by design, and the paper’s patient random-effect does not model TMT batch explicitly.

Effect on the claim. When the top differential proteins are re-tested with TMT set as an explicit random effect, 13 of 15 remain significant (several at p < 1e-6); only ERGIC1 and FAH lose significance. As a positive control, the within-patient cancer-vs-normal contrast retains ~103% of its effect within batch — confirming the test preserves genuine biology and specifically isolates the batch-entangled between-patient contrasts. The differential signatures hold; the honest caveat is that a handful of between-patient proteins are batch-fragile and the design cannot fully disentangle the two.

Caveat 2 — A few headline differential proteins rest on heavily imputed values
VERIFIED NO CLAIM REVERSAL
ClaimNamed TNBC-vs-HR+ differential proteins (Supplementary Data 2F) TestPer-subtype missingness in the raw (pre-imputation) matrix S9A vs the imputed analysis matrix S9B

The analysis matrix is filtered to ≥50% valid values then KNN-imputed. For the top 20 differential proteins, median per-subtype missingness is 0% — the vast majority are fully measured and their fold-changes are genuine. But three are fragile: KRT8 (FDR 7.6e-19) is 93–94% missing in both subtypes, so its fold-change is computed almost entirely from imputed values; PSAT1 has 43% missingness in HR+ (differential imputation); CHMP4A is majority-imputed in TNBC.

Effect on the claim. The overall differential signature is sound (most proteins fully measured). The caveat is protein-specific: a small number of named differential proteins — KRT8 most notably — should carry a “low-coverage / imputation-dependent” flag and not be over-interpreted individually.

Caveat 3 — ~4% of Grade-differential “proteins” are ambiguous multi-gene groups
VERIFIED DISCLOSED / MINOR
ClaimNamed differential proteins (Supplementary Data 2C / 2F) TestComposite-identifier cross-reference against the significant-protein lists

178 rows of the matrix carry composite multi-gene identifiers (shared-peptide protein groups, e.g. HPCAL1;HPCA, UBE2N;UBE2NL, CKMT1A;CKMT1B). Of the published significant differentials, 41 of 1,064 (Grade 3-vs-2) and 1 of 32 (TNBC-vs-HR+) are such composites; cancer-vs-normal has none. For these, the peptides cannot distinguish the constituent genes, so “gene X is differential” is really “this protein group is differential.”

Effect on the claim. Minor and transparent — the paper reports the composite names verbatim in its supplementary tables, so the ambiguity is disclosed rather than hidden. It is a standard mass-spectrometry limitation. It would only become a defect if a single-gene biological claim were attached to one of these groups.

Caveat 4 — The proteomic-ITH×grade result is real and unconfounded, but statistically borderline
VERIFIED NO CLAIM REVERSAL
ClaimFig 2H — higher proteomic intra-tumor heterogeneity (P-ITH) in Grade 3 TestP-ITH vs regions-per-group correlation; grade-balance of region counts; batch scope

P-ITH is computed within a single TMT set per patient, which already controls the batch confound, and over balanced region groups (4–9 regions). We confirmed P-ITH does not track region count (Spearman ρ = +0.06, p = 0.76 — no sampling artifact) and that region counts are balanced across grades (p = 0.25 — no confound). The Grade 3 > Grade 2 difference is genuine (0.178 vs 0.122).

Effect on the claim. The claim is methodologically sound and survives every confound we could test. The only caveat is statistical: the grade difference is borderline (Mann–Whitney p = 0.048) on a small sample (n = 16 vs 12) — robust in design, fragile in power.

Deposit re-use caveats (verified; relevant only to re-analysts, not to the paper)

These are real properties of the public data deposit that would bite a downstream re-user, but do not touch any published result — we confirmed each does not reach the paper’s figures.

Re-use 1 — QuPath flat export interleaves roll-up parents with detail rows
VERIFIED RE-USE ONLY
WhereDeposited *_annotations.csv; SET1-FullAnalysis.groovy:207–208 Paper impactNone — published analysis uses per-region densities, not raw sums

Each deposited QuPath annotation table mixes whole-tissue roll-up rows (WholeTissue/ExactTissue) with their child region and cell rows in one flat table, with no level column to distinguish them (the intended WholeTissue exclusion sits commented out in the export script). A naive SUM over area or detection counts over-counts by up to ~7.9×.

Effect on the paper. None. The published imaging readout uses per-region cell densities (cells_per_mm²) and ratios (BV %), which do not sum across the annotation hierarchy. The hazard is purely for re-analysts who load the flat export and aggregate naively; they should filter to one annotation level first.

Re-use 2 — Two slides are missing an entire imaging panel (blank ≠ zero)
VERIFIED RE-USE ONLY
WhereSlide BC24_1-5 (no Set2/immune panel); BC7_1-4 (no blood-vessel output) Paper impactNone — covered by adjacent serial sections

One slide is missing its entire immune (Set2) panel and another its blood-vessel classifier output; the missing values are stored as blank, not zero, so a naive fillna(0) would silently under-report immune density / vascular coverage for those areas.

Effect on the paper. None. Each tumor region is imaged across multiple consecutive sections, so a region whose panel is missing on one slide is captured on a sibling slide; the published per-region table (Supplementary Data 6) is complete across all 121 regions. The hazard is for re-users working at the slide grain.

Reading guide

This report is deliberately a negative result: a rigorous, localized audit that found no claim-invalidating defect in a well-constructed paper. The four claim-level items are bounded caveats — the kind of limitation that belongs in a careful reader’s mental footnotes, not a correction. Two of them (batch confounding, proteomic-ITH) the authors already mitigate by design (within-TMT-set computation, patient random effects); the other two (imputation fragility of a few proteins, composite-gene ambiguity) are inherent mass-spectrometry limitations that the deposited tables disclose.

The two re-use caveats are real properties of the public deposit, not the paper: they would mislead a downstream re-analyst who aggregates the raw QuPath export naively, but we verified neither reaches the published figures.

The methodological point is the durable one. Rather than attempt an exhaustive figure-by-figure reproduction (impossible here, since the statistical code was not deposited), the audit used semantic enrichment of the deposited data to localize the ~8 places where a claim could plausibly be at risk, then tested each one. Every check resolved in the paper’s favor. The dataset and code are authentic, and the paper’s conclusions are sound.
