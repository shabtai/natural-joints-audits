LORIS — Audit Report

Generated: 2026-04-29  |  Dataset: LORIS bundle (Chowell train/test, MSK1, MSK2, Kato pan-cancer, Ravi NSCLC, Vanguri NSCLC, MSK_nonICB)  |  Domain: Immune-checkpoint blockade response prediction  |  Code: LORIS/code/*.py, *.Rmd  |  Data: Zenodo deposit 11186449  |  Paper: Chang et al., Nature Cancer (2024)
Audit by Natural Joints
Audit design: Multi-agent audit covering code, data honesty, and data facts, followed by independent verification, public-record novelty checking, and impact quantification (rerun where possible, otherwise proxy). The full audit produced a larger set of findings; this report covers only those whose impact on the paper’s reported results is rated MAJOR or MODERATE.

Headline: Three findings undermine claims that the paper rests on rather than the AUC values reported. Three further findings change the interpretation or precision of those AUCs without changing their direction. None of the issues require the model itself to be wrong; several show that specific cohort-level claims of generalization are not supported by the deposited inputs.
Numerical caveat. Where AUC deltas are quoted below, they come from focused reproductions on the deposited Zenodo data using 200 bootstrap resamples (the paper’s pipelines use 10,000). Treat the magnitudes as estimates with non-trivial Monte Carlo error; treat the directions and the structural claims as the load-bearing parts.

Major findings

Finding 1 — MSK2 cancer-type vector is identically zero for the LLR6_pan model
VERIFIED NOVEL* MAJOR
Rule typeMathematically inert feature on a headline cohort Location12.Formula_LORIS.py (featuresNA); data/MSK2.csv

The pan-cancer LLR6 model has 21 features: TMB, Systemic_therapy_history, Albumin, NLR, Age, and one-hot indicators CancerType1…CancerType16. The deposited MSK2.csv contains 104 patients in two cancer-type classes only:

  • CancerType17 (Unknown primary): 29 patients
  • CancerType18 (CNS): 75 patients
  • CancerType1…CancerType16: all zero in every row

Because the model has no slot for CancerType17 or CancerType18, the cancer-type contribution to every MSK2 prediction is exactly zero. The LLR6_pan score on MSK2 is therefore a function of TMB, Albumin, NLR, Age, and Systemic_therapy_history alone — a 5-feature score, not a pan-cancer one.

A repo-wide grep across the 37 deposited scripts finds zero references to CancerType17 or CancerType18 anywhere — no remap, no fallback bucket, no per-cohort retraining. The coefficient file PanCancer_LLR6_10k_ParamCalculate.txt is fixed length 21 (5 continuous + CT1..CT16).

Effect on paper claims. MSK2 is presented in the manuscript as evidence that LLR6 generalizes to cancer types not seen during training. Whatever signal the reported MSK2 AUC carries, it cannot be evidence that the cancer-type representation generalizes — that part of the model is mathematically silent on this cohort. The claim of pan-cancer generalization to novel cancer types is not supported by the deposited model architecture and the deposited test data, regardless of the AUC value.

Finding 2 — NSCLC LLR6 is fit on the concatenation of Chowell_train + Chowell_test
VERIFIED NOVEL* MAJOR
Rule typeTrain/test contamination (parameter estimation step) Location08_4.NSCLC_LLRx_10k_ParamCalculate.py:47–50

The script that estimates the NSCLC-specific LLR6 coefficients begins by concatenating the held-out test split into the training pool, before applying the NSCLC filter:

data_train = pd.read_excel(input_xlsx, sheet_name='Chowell_train')
data_test  = pd.read_excel(input_xlsx, sheet_name='Chowell_test')
data_all   = pd.concat([data_train, data_test], axis=0)
data_NSCLC = data_all[data_all['CancerType_grouped'] == 'NSCLC']

The pan-cancer counterpart (05_5.PanCancer_LLRx_ParamCalculate.py) fits the LLR6_pan model on Chowell_train alone — the methodology silently diverges between the two model families.

The manuscript reports training the NSCLC model on “324 NSCLC participants”. The concatenated NSCLC subset in the deposited inputs is approximately 538 rows. The number reported in the paper text and the number actually used in the deposited code do not agree.

Effect on paper claims. Reproduction on the train-only NSCLC subset (200 resamples) gives an AUC drop on Chowell-NSCLC of approximately 0.789 → 0.759 (Δ ≈ −0.03), the same scale as the LLR6-vs-TMB superiority margin claimed in the paper. The qualitative direction of NSCLC superiority over TMB survives at the lower estimate, but the headline gap is partly an artefact of the inflated fit. Every downstream NSCLC figure that uses the same coefficient file (Fig 6, Fig 10, Fig 11; scripts 09_3…09_5) inherits the same compromised estimate.

Finding 3 — Albumin and NLR are constants in Kato_panCancer and Ravi_NSCLC; LLR6 reduces to LLR4 on these cohorts
VERIFIED NOVEL* MAJOR
Rule typeDegenerate features on validation cohort (data honesty) Locationdata/Kato_panCancer.csv; data/Ravi_NSCLC.csv

In both cohorts, two of the six LLR6 input features take a single value across every patient:

  • Albumin = 3.8 g/dL on every row
  • NLR = 6.2 on every row

The LLR6 linear predictor on these cohorts therefore decomposes into a per-patient term that uses only TMB, Systemic_therapy_history, Age (and CancerType for Kato) plus a fixed cohort-level offset from the imputed Albumin and NLR values. Within either cohort, no patient-to-patient variation in score comes from Albumin or NLR; the ROC, Cox, and threshold analyses on these cohorts measure a 4-feature model dressed as a 6-feature model.

Effect on paper claims. Kato and Ravi are presented as independent validation that LLR6 generalizes the joint use of all six features. On the deposited inputs that joint use is mathematically impossible for these cohorts — Albumin and NLR contribute zero discriminative information patient-to-patient. The numerical AUC values do not change (the constants merely shift the score by a fixed amount, which ranking-based AUC ignores), but the claim of feature-set generalization is structurally identical to F1 (MSK2): the reported AUC is real, what it measures is not what the manuscript implies.

Moderate findings

Finding 4 — Drop-censored landmark labels in OS-at-T ROC inflate the AUC
VERIFIED NOVEL* MODERATE
Rule typeInformative censoring → biased landmark labels Location07_3 PanCancer_LORIS_ROC_AUC_nonICB_vs_ICB_OS.py:92–103

The OS-at-time-T binary outcome used for the ROC analyses is constructed as:

label = NaN
label[(OS_Months <= T) & (Event == 1)] = 1   # died by T
label[OS_Months >  T]                  = 0   # alive at T
# patients with OS_Months <= T and Event == 0 stay NaN and are dropped

Patients censored before time T — the cases with the most ambiguous outcome — are silently removed from the AUC computation rather than handled with a Kaplan-Meier-based AUCt. The retained sample is enriched for patients with definitive outcomes, which makes the discrimination task easier than the question the paper asks.

Effect on paper claims. Re-computing the AUC at the same horizon using a censoring-aware AUCt (Heagerty’s method) on the deposited Chowell_test data shifts the pan-cancer OS AUC by approximately −0.03 — the same magnitude as the LORIS-over-TMB margin and the LORIS-over-TIDE margin emphasised in the paper. The qualitative ordering of LORIS vs. baselines survives the correction; the size of the win shrinks. The construction also affects the predictive-vs-prognostic comparison, because ICB and non-ICB cohorts have different censoring distributions and therefore different magnitudes of inflation.

Finding 5 — Independence of “external” MSK validation cohorts is overstated; populations overlap with the Chowell test set
PARTIALLY VERIFIED NOVEL* MODERATE
Rule typeExternal-validation framing / population independence LocationCohort design — conversion_manifest.json; sheet structure of AllData.xlsx

The manuscript frames Chowell_test, MSK1, and MSK2 as independent test sets used for external validation. The deposited provenance documents that all three derive from MSK Cancer Center clinical-sequencing cohorts with overlapping enrollment windows, and that patients in the Chowell test split are drawn from the same MSK-IMPACT registry from which MSK1 and MSK2 are sampled.

This is not a code defect — no row in the deposited CSVs is duplicated across files — but the populations are institutionally and temporally entangled. The kinds of confounders that “external” validation is supposed to stress-test (different sequencing platform, different sample-handling, different clinical workflow, different selection biases) are not exercised by these splits.

Effect on paper claims. The strongest reading of the paper—LORIS is a model that travels across institutions and platforms—is not supported by these particular cohorts. The narrower reading—LORIS holds up across temporally and structurally separated subcohorts of MSK-IMPACT—remains supported. The numerical AUCs are unchanged; the generalization claim attached to them needs to be rescaled.

Finding 6 — The custom DeLong test omits the covariance term, biasing every paired-AUC p-value
VERIFIED NOVEL* MODERATE
Rule typeWrong variance formula in significance test Location06_5.PanCancer_vs_nonNSCLC_LLR6_ROC_AUC.py:30–40 (and re-used downstream)

The function used throughout the paper to compare two AUCs measured on the same patients is named delong_test but implements the Hanley–McNeil variance for a single AUC twice, summing the variances without subtracting the covariance term that DeLong’s actual paired test computes from concordant tied-rank counts.

var_diff = var_A + var_B            # missing -2 * cov(A, B)

For two AUCs computed on the same set of patients (which is the situation everywhere this function is called — LORIS vs. TMB, LORIS vs. TIDE, LLR6 vs. RF6, etc.) the AUC errors are positively correlated; ignoring the covariance over-estimates Var(A−B) and produces p-values that are systematically too large.

Effect on paper claims. The bias direction is conservative for the paper’s positive results — significant differences remain significant under a correctly implemented DeLong test. The bias does, however, mean that every reported p-value comparing two AUCs in the manuscript and supplements is an over-estimate; readers comparing the precise reported values to other studies that use a correct paired test will see numerically different (smaller) p-values on a re-run. Downstream, this matters for any “LORIS not significantly worse than X” claim, where the conservative test makes non-inferiority easier to declare than it should be.

Reading guide

The three MAJOR findings are not claims that LORIS’s reported AUCs are wrong. They are claims that three of the structural generalization arguments the paper builds on top of those AUCs — pan-cancer transfer to novel cancer types (MSK2), NSCLC-specific superiority (clean train/test separation), and 6-feature generalization to Kato/Ravi — are unsupported on the deposited inputs in their current form. The remaining MODERATE findings shift specific numerical comparisons (the predictive-vs-prognostic AUC margin, the precision of paired-AUC p-values) and a framing claim (“external” vs. within-MSK validation) without overturning the model’s direction of effect.

Findings rated MINOR or NEGLIGIBLE by the impact quantifier are not reproduced here per scope. They cover code-style issues (hardcoded model selection that ignores a CLI argument, an unused worksheet name, a search over a 1-point hyperparameter grid), data-quality blemishes (a TMB cap at 50 in MSK_nonICB, a 39% NaN drop in Vanguri’s OS_Event that does not move the AUC, an OS scale apparently in days rather than months in Ravi that does not affect Cox), and statistical-design choices that change the interpretation of supplementary tables but not the headline conclusions.
