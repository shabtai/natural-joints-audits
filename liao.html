Liao 2020 BALF COVID-19 — Audit Report

Generated: 2026-04-30  |  Dataset: 13 BAL samples (4 healthy controls, 3 mild, 6 severe COVID-19), 65,813 cells  |  Domain: Single-cell RNA-seq of bronchoalveolar lavage in COVID-19  |  Code: zhangzlab/covid_balf  |  Data: UCSC Cell Browser  |  Paper: Liao et al., Nature Medicine (2020)
Audit by Natural Joints
Audit design: Multi-agent audit covering code, data honesty, and data facts, followed by independent verification, public-record novelty checking, and impact quantification on the deposited inputs. The full audit produced eighteen findings across reproducibility, transparency, and statistical inference; this report covers only the four whose impact is to invalidate or substantially weaken specific claims in the paper. Reproducibility-only failures (deposited code that cannot regenerate a published table or figure), naming-collision risks, and latent bugs that no script in the deposited code currently activates are excluded from this report.

Headline: The paper’s direction-of-effect findings on macrophage compositional shift, T-cell exhaustion markers, and cytokine differences across severity may well be real, but the statistical support for them as published is much weaker than the reported p-values suggest. The cohort structure is dominated by two severe patients and one healthy-control sample of different provenance; cell-level p-values inflate group-comparison significance by roughly one hundred orders of magnitude relative to patient-level inference on the same data.
Numerical caveat. The integrated Seurat object (nCoV.rds) was not re-fit during this audit; AUC-style or DE-effect-size deltas would require regenerating that object end-to-end. Quantifications below come from the released per-cell annotation tables (all.cell.annotation.meta.txt, myeloid.cell.annotation.meta.txt, NKT.cell.annotation.meta.txt) and the sample-level meta.txt. Treat the p-value comparison in Finding 1 as a proxy on the released composition counts; treat the cohort-structure facts in Findings 2–4 as exact.

Major findings

Finding 1 — Cell-level differential expression and composition tests treat 65,813 cells as 65,813 independent samples, but the study has 13 patients
VERIFIED NOVEL* MAJOR
Rule typePseudoreplication — wrong unit of statistical analysis Locationtotal_fig.R:178–204; cytokine_fig.R:68–79; macrophage_fig.R:74–78, 144–151; NKT.R:78–91, 156, 248–250

Throughout the analysis, group comparisons (HC vs Mild vs Severe) are run with FindAllMarkers(..., test.use='MAST') after setting Idents(...)='group', so every cell becomes one observation labeled by its patient’s clinical group. The composition figure does the same with a per-cluster Welch t-test:

my_comparisons <- list(c("HC","M"), c("M","S"), c("HC","S"))
pp1 + stat_compare_means(comparisons = my_comparisons, method = "t.test")

Cells from the same patient are correlated — same genetic background, same infection state, same library prep. The correct unit of analysis is the patient (n = 4 / 3 / 6), not the cell. The authors knew this: NKT.R:260 is the only DE call in the entire codebase that includes latent.vars='orig.ident', and it is used for exactly one cell type (CD8 T markers). Every other DE call — cytokine signatures, macrophage subgroup markers, NKT proliferation, all severity contrasts — omits the patient covariate.

On the released composition counts, the macrophage Mild vs Severe proportion contrast gives:

  • Cell-level χ2: p ≈ 2 × 10−107
  • Patient-level Mann–Whitney on per-sample percentages: p ≈ 0.55

The gap is approximately 106 orders of magnitude in −log10(p). Cell-level inference systematically inflates significance by roughly √(Ncells / Npatients) in the test statistic, which compounds exponentially in the p-value.

Effect on paper claims. Direction-of-effect statements about macrophage compositional shifts, cytokine signatures, and severity-stratified DE in the paper may be biologically real, but the statistical support reported alongside them does not survive a patient-level analysis with n = 4 / 3 / 6. The headline p-values are not credible at face value, and any claim that depends on a small p-value to clear a multiple-testing threshold needs to be re-evaluated under proper pseudobulk DE (DESeq2/limma per patient×cell-type) or a mixed model with patient as a random effect.

Finding 2 — HC4 (GSM3660650) is an external GEO sample with 51% epithelial composition; integrated into the healthy baseline with no batch covariate
VERIFIED NOVEL* MAJOR
Rule typeCohort heterogeneity — HC reference dominated by one out-of-protocol sample Locationmeta.txt row 4 (sample = GSM3660650); seurat_integration.R — vars.to.regress = c("nCount_RNA","percent.mito"), no orig.ident or study covariate

Three of the four healthy controls (C51, C52, C100) are in-house BAL samples. The fourth, GSM3660650, is a public GEO accession from a different study with a different processing protocol. The composition speaks for itself:

The mean HC epithelial fraction is 13.0% with HC4 included, 0.4% without — a roughly 32-fold inflation driven by a single sample. Mean macrophage fraction shifts from 89.6% to 73.5% by the same mechanism. seurat_integration.R regresses out nCount_RNA and percent.mito in ScaleData; it does not include sample, study, or protocol as a covariate at any stage. CCA-anchor integration (FindIntegrationAnchors at line 137) cannot distinguish “remove technical batch noise” from “remove HC4-specific biology” when HC4 is the only carrier of a real signal in the HC arm.

GitHub issue #22 on the deposited repository contains a partial, informal acknowledgment from the authors that healthy samples were CD45+-enriched while disease samples were total cells — an admission that HC and disease arms are not directly comparable on cell-type proportions. This caveat appears nowhere in the paper text or figure legends.

Effect on paper claims. Any composition comparison that uses HC as a baseline — epithelial expansion in COVID, macrophage depletion in healthy, T-cell prevalence vs healthy — has an effective HC sample size of n ≈ 1 for the epithelial dimension and is partially HC4-driven for the macrophage dimension. “Epithelial cells appear in COVID lung and not in healthy” reduces, on the deposited inputs, to a comparison against three CD45+-enriched samples plus one out-of-protocol sample. The structural argument that HC4 makes the HC arm comparable to the COVID arm is unsupported.

The published narrative treats severe COVID as an n = 6 group. At the cell level — which is the level at which DE, marker tests, and cytokine signatures are computed (see Finding 1) — severe COVID is essentially two patients. S2 alone contributes 46% of the cellular evidence; the four lowest-cell-count severe patients combined contribute 22%.

The within-severe heterogeneity is real biology: viral load (nCoV_mean in meta.txt) ranges from 0.003 (S4) to 0.750 (S3) across the six patients — a 250-fold spread — and macrophage subgroup composition varies markedly across severe patients (per the released myeloid.cell.annotation.meta.txt Group1–4 counts).

Effect on paper claims. Combined with Finding 1, every “severe COVID” cell-level finding is essentially a two-patient finding generalised to a six-patient cohort. The macrophage inflammatory shift, the cytokine signature, and the T-cell exhaustion profile reported for severe disease all need to be qualified as “observed in two of six severe patients with very high cell yield, with limited cellular evidence from the remaining four.” This is the same n-effective ≈ 1–2 problem as Finding 2, on the disease side.

M3 is consistent with sample-quality issues acknowledged informally by the authors in repo issue #6 (“for M3, the sample quality is not so good and we harvest fewer cells in our experiment”). The statistical-power consequence is not discussed anywhere in the published paper. With n = 3 in the mild arm, M3 is leverage-dominant on every per-sample average:

  • Macrophage proportion variance with M3 included: 0.0281; without M3: 0.0001 — M3 inflates within-mild variance ~280×.
  • Per-sample cell-type proportion estimates from M3 carry ±~5% Monte Carlo noise just from the small denominator, which is comparable to the reported HC-vs-Mild and Mild-vs-Severe effect sizes.

Effect on paper claims. Any per-sample comparison involving the mild arm (composition Welch t-tests in total_fig.R:178–204; per-cluster mild-vs-X breakdowns) is dominated by M1 and M2, with M3 either dragging the mean toward zero or inflating variance enough to suppress significance. The paper’s “mild as an intermediate phenotype between HC and severe” framing rests on a 2-patient effective sample, not 3.

Reading guide

The four MAJOR findings are not claims that the deposited cellular annotations are incorrect, that the macrophage subgroups don’t exist, or that severe COVID does not differ from healthy lung at the molecular level. They are claims that the statistical inference structure the paper builds on top of those cellular observations does not survive proper patient-level analysis on the released cohort. The direction of the reported effects may well replicate; the p-values, the “n = 6 severe / n = 3 mild” framing, and the comparability of the HC arm to the disease arms do not.

The four findings are not independent: they compound. Finding 1 says cell-level p-values are inflated by ~100 orders of magnitude relative to patient-level inference. Findings 2–4 say the patient-level n on which a corrected analysis would have to rest is closer to 1–2 per arm than the reported 4 / 3 / 6. The combined consequence is that none of the headline severity-axis claims (macrophage shift, cytokine signature, T-cell exhaustion) carries the level of statistical support a reader would infer from the reported significance levels.

Findings rated MODERATE, MINOR, or NEGLIGIBLE by the impact quantifier are not reproduced here per scope. They include reproducibility failures (deposited code that cannot regenerate published outputs — subset filters on group values that do not exist in the data; a UMI threshold variable that references a column name that does not exist; a label-rename mismatch that drops macrophages from a composition figure; a script that uses a variable before it is defined; a TSV round-trip that drops sample/group columns), transparency issues (the “myeloid” annotation file released with the paper contains only macrophages with anonymised Group1–4 labels; the healthy-control viral-read counts are hardcoded to zero rather than measured), latent bugs that no script in the deposited code currently activates (a per-cell viral-detection column treated by future re-users as patient-level; a no-op doublet-removal step on an upstream-deleted cluster; a string-mismatch filter that leaves 189 “Uncertain” cells in the NKT analysis), and naming-collision risks that do not produce numerical errors in the deposited code (label vocabularies O1–3 / C1–5 overlapping with original sample IDs C100, C141, etc.). These are documented in the audit transcript but do not, on their own, invalidate any paper-level claim.
